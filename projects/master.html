Master’s Thesis

LULC Classification using Machine Learning and Deep Learning algorithms with the aid of Google Earth Engine

Land Use and Land Cover (LULC) Classification is a crucial process for natural resource management and environmental analysis. This study explores the application of Machine Learning and Deep Learning techniques to automate LULC classification, with a particular focus on vegetation recognition and verifying land use declarations made by farmers within the framework of European funding programs.

The European Union annually allocates agricultural funds through programs such as FEAGA and FEASR, aimed at supporting sustainable farming practices. However, the verification of land use declarations has traditionally been a manual process, constrained by time and resource limitations. This study proposes an automated approach leveraging satellite imagery obtained from Google Earth Engine, combined with Random Forest, eXtreme Gradient Boosting (XGBoost), and Artificial Neural Networks.

The project involved acquiring geospatial data from various sources, including public datasets from ISPRA, and preprocessing them to extract meaningful features from Sentinel-2 satellite images elaborated from Google Earth Engine. To enhance classification accuracy, spectral indices such as NDVI (Normalized Difference Vegetation Index) and EVI (Enhanced Vegetation Index) were computed.

Process of image acquisition from Sentinel-2 and conversion to numerical values

The models were trained to address three main tasks:

  • Vegetation recognition: Differentiating between vegetated and non-vegetated areas withhigh accuracy.
  • Vegetation classification: Identifying specific categories such as olive groves, vineyards, conifer plantations, and orchards.
  • Detailed vegetation classification: Refining classification to distinguish various crop types, improving the precision of vegetation feature identification.
Additionally, an experiment was conducted using synthetic data, generated to simulate fraudulent land use declarations. The results demonstrated that the models could be further refined to detect anomalies in the data, improving the robustness of the classification system.

Results obtained for different problems described with different algorithms used

Overall, the results confirm that Machine Learning can serve as a powerful tool for automated land use classification, enabling faster and more accurate verification of agricultural declarations. Future research could focus on integrating new models and utilizing high- resolution multispectral data to further enhance performance.
